1Executive summary

RadShield is a software tool that predicts how much gamma radiation a person would receive at a given location, given the radioactive sources present and the shielding (tank walls, lead, steel, water, concrete) between them and that location. It answers everyday radiological-planning questions — "how much dose at the tank wall?", "how thick does the lead need to be?", "what will the operator accumulate during this transfer?" — from a 3-D model you build on screen.

The important point for anyone evaluating whether to rely on it: RadShield is not an ad-hoc estimator. It implements the same calculation method, and uses the same nationally standardized reference data, as the established professional shielding codes used across the nuclear industry. Specifically:

  • The method is the classical point-kernel with buildup factors — the documented method family behind codes such as MicroShield® (see §3).
  • The shielding "buildup" data comes straight from the national consensus standard ANSI/ANS-6.4.3-1991, Gamma-Ray Attenuation Coefficients and Buildup Factors for Engineering Materials (see §4).
  • The photon attenuation data comes from the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST XCOM), the reference database for these coefficients (see Appendix D).
  • It has been checked against the standard's own numbers. RadShield's buildup calculations reproduce the values tabulated in ANSI/ANS-6.4.3 to within 2% across 28 independent test cases, and a suite of over 100 automated tests guards the geometry, physics, and bookkeeping (see §5).

Compared with the quick hand-calculations and generic charts often used for a first estimate, RadShield's advantage is fidelity: it accounts for the true 3-D geometry of the source and every shield layer along each line of sight, for the scattered radiation that simple attenuation formulas ignore, and for multiple sources and time-varying activity at once. It produces a documented, repeatable number and a records-quality report — not a back-of-envelope figure (§2).

Bottom line. RadShield brings the rigor of a professional shielding code — standardized method, standardized data, verified against the standard — to routine dose-rate estimates, in a tool that models the actual geometry of the job. It is a deterministic engineering estimate, not a measurement, and its limitations are stated plainly in §7.

2Where RadShield fits current practice

Most routine dose-rate estimates are made one of three ways. Each has a place; each also has a ceiling that RadShield is designed to lift.

Common methodWhat it capturesWhere it falls short
Point-source hand calc
(Ẋ = Γ·A / d²)		 A fast bare-source estimate from the isotope's gamma constant and distance. No shielding, no source size, no geometry. Useful only as an unshielded upper bound.
Attenuation hand calc
(add e−μx)		 Adds one shield layer via exponential attenuation. Ignores buildup (scattered photons), so it under-predicts; awkward for multiple layers, real geometry, or multiple sources.
Charts / nomographs Pre-computed dose-vs-thickness curves for standard cases. Only as good as the match between your job and the chart's assumed geometry.

RadShield keeps the same physics these methods approximate, then removes their simplifications: it integrates over the actual size and shape of the source, traces each line of sight through every shield layer it truly crosses, and applies the standardized buildup factors that account for scattered radiation the simple attenuation formula leaves out. It handles multiple sources of different isotopes in one scene and can integrate dose over a time-varying evolution. The result is the same kind of number a dedicated shielding code produces, with a documented basis you can attach to a work package.

RadShield complements, not replaces, measurement. It is an engineering prediction used for planning and ALARA optimization. Field survey remains the system of record; RadShield's value is telling you before the job where the dose will be and what shielding will do about it.

3How a dose rate is calculated

RadShield uses the point-kernel method. The idea is simple to state: break the radioactive source into many tiny pieces, work out the contribution of each piece at the point of interest, and add them all up. The subtlety — and where the standardized data earns its keep — is in the middle step.

3.1  The five stages in plain language

  1. Mesh the source. A real source has size — the activity is spread through a volume of liquid, sludge, or metal. RadShield subdivides it into hundreds of small elements, each carrying its share of the activity, so the source's extent and its self-shielding (radiation absorbed on the way out of the source itself) are included, not ignored. (Appendix A, §A.4.)
  2. Trace the line of sight. From each element, RadShield draws a straight ray to the point of interest and records exactly which materials it passes through and how much of each — water, then steel, then lead, then air, and so on (see Figure 2). This is pure geometry, done analytically for each shape (Appendix A, §A.3).
  3. Attenuate. Two well-known physical laws are applied along that ray: radiation spreads out with distance (the inverse-square law), and each shield layer removes photons exponentially according to its material and thickness. The strength of each material's absorption comes from NIST reference data (Appendix C, Steps 2–3).
  4. Add buildup. Simple exponential attenuation counts only photons that reach the point without ever scattering. In a real shield, many photons Compton-scatter and still arrive, just with less energy. The buildup factor corrects for exactly this. RadShield's buildup factors are the tabulated coefficients from ANSI/ANS-6.4.3-1991 — this is the single most important reason its results are more realistic than a bare attenuation calculation (Appendix B).
  5. Convert and sum. The resulting radiation field is converted to a dose rate in mrem/hr using the energy-absorption response of air, and the contributions of every source element and every gamma-ray energy line of the isotope are added together (Appendix C, Steps 6–7).
Why buildup matters to the answer. Through several mean free paths of shielding, scattered photons can multiply the "uncollided" dose many times over — for water at 1 MeV and 10 mean free paths the buildup factor is about 26 (Appendix B). A calculation that omits buildup can therefore be low by more than an order of magnitude through a thick shield. Getting this term right, from the recognized standard, is central to a defensible result.

4.1  About ANSI/ANS-6.4.3-1991

ANSI/ANS-6.4.3-1991, Gamma-Ray Attenuation Coefficients and Buildup Factors for Engineering Materials, is the American National Standard — issued by the American Nuclear Society and approved by ANSI — that tabulates the buildup factors used throughout shielding engineering. It is the same reference data set that underlies commercial point-kernel codes. RadShield uses the standard's Geometric-Progression (GP) coefficient fits (its Table 5.1) to reconstruct the buildup factor at any depth and energy, and validates the reconstruction against the standard's own directly tabulated buildup factors (its Table 3). The mathematics is in Appendix B; the validation is in §5.

Why this is persuasive. When a tool's buildup factors are the standard's coefficients, and its reconstruction of those factors matches the standard's published table to within 2%, the shielding term — historically the hardest part of a hand calculation to get right — is on the same footing as a professional code.

6What RadShield can model

The same calculation engine drives several planning tools:

  • Arbitrary 3-D scenes — cylinders, boxes, plates, disks, pipes/annuli, and spheres, in any position and orientation, with steel, lead, water, concrete, and air; multiple sources of different isotopes in one scene.
  • Named dose points — evaluate any number of locations at once, with a results table and a printable dose-assessment report.
  • Isodose surfaces — the "100 mrem/hr boundary" drawn in 3-D around each source, exactly as a survey would find it (Figure 4; method in Appendix E, §E.1).
  • Survey heat map — the dose field painted across a horizontal plane, like a posted survey map, computed instead of measured (Appendix E, §E.2).
  • Parameter sweep — "how thick does the lead need to be?" answered as a dose-vs-thickness curve (Appendix E, §E.3).
  • Time simulation — transfers, discharges, and spills over time, giving the peak dose rate and the integrated dose a worker would accumulate over an evolution (Appendix E, §E.4).

7Honest limitations

A tool is trustworthy partly because it is candid about what it does not do. RadShield is a point-kernel estimator, not a full radiation-transport simulation. The following are stated plainly; the complete engineering list is in Appendix F.

  • It is a deterministic estimate, not a measurement. Use it for planning and ALARA; the field survey remains the record.
  • Point-kernel + buildup, not transport. Buildup factors assume an idealized infinite medium. Genuinely three-dimensional scatter effects — radiation streaming through gaps, skyshine, duct/maze scatter, sharp shield-edge effects — are not explicitly modeled. Near such features the estimate can err in either direction; a transport code (e.g., MCNP) is the right tool there.
  • Layered-shield buildup uses an engineering convention (the "outermost material" rule, Appendix B, §B.3) — the same approximation standard point-kernel codes use. It is least accurate for a thin high-Z liner over a thick low-Z shield, where it tends to under-predict buildup.
  • Gamma only. Beta, neutron, bremsstrahlung, and characteristic X-ray contributions are not modeled; the response is exposure-in-air (mrad ≈ mrem for photons), not an ICRP organ/effective dose.
  • Bundled materials and isotopes. Five materials at fixed composition (steel is modeled as pure iron), and a fixed isotope library (Co-60, Cs-137, Ir-192, I-131, Na-24). See Appendix D.
Use within its envelope. For routine shielded-source and layered-wall geometry — tanks, bottles, plates, pipe runs — RadShield is squarely in the regime where the point-kernel method is accurate and well-established. For streaming, skyshine, or heavily non-convex "shadow" geometry, treat its numbers as indicative and confirm with survey or a transport calculation.

8Conclusion

RadShield puts a standards-based shielding calculation — the same method family and the same national reference data used by professional codes — into a tool that models the real 3-D geometry of the work. Its buildup physics reproduces the ANSI/ANS-6.4.3 standard to within 2%, its full computational chain is under continuous automated verification, and its assumptions and limits are documented. For the planning of routine shielded-source work it is a defensible, repeatable, and auditable engineering estimate — a meaningful step up in fidelity from point-source and single-layer hand calculations, delivered in a tool that requires no installation and produces a records-quality report.

Appendix AGeometry engine & ray tracing

RadShield represents a scene as a set of analytic solid volumes. Each volume is defined in its own local frame (axis along +Y, bottom-center at the origin); world↔local transforms handle position and rotation, so the shape mathematics never has to deal with orientation. Rotation is Euler angles in degrees applied in three.js 'XYZ' order, and the physics engine builds the identical rotation matrix, so the picture and the calculation always agree.

A.1  Volume types

ClassShapeRay-intersection method
CylinderSolid cylinder (radius, height)Quadratic for the side wall + two cap planes
AnnulusHollow cylinder (inner/outer radius, height)Quadratics for both walls + annular caps
DiskThin cylinder (radius, thickness)As cylinder
BoxRectangular solid (width X, depth Z, height Y)Slab method (three axis-aligned slab intervals intersected)
SphereSolid sphere (radius; center at +radius above the position)Quadratic

Each volume carries a role (source / shield / container), a material, a priority, a display-only visibility flag, and an enabled flag. A volume with enabled = false is excluded from every physics query — ray tracing, material lookup, meshing, and activity totals — which drives the "what is this shield worth?" what-if workflow.

A.2  Material at a point — the priority rule

To decide the material at any point, RadShield asks every enabled volume whether it contains that point; among those that do, the one with the highest priority wins; if none contain it, the material is air. This is how overlapping shapes compose: a plug (priority 55) inside a lid opening (priority 50) reads as plug material; a source disk (priority 20) inside the water column (priority 10) reads as source.

A.3  Ray tracing

To trace a ray from a source element to a dose point, RadShield:

  1. Collects the parametric distances of every surface crossing of every enabled volume (each shape's analytic intersection), plus the two endpoints.
  2. Sorts those boundaries. Between consecutive boundaries the material cannot change, so it samples the midpoint of each segment with the priority rule.
  3. Merges adjacent same-material segments, yielding an ordered list of {material, thickness} from source to dose point — including air gaps and the source's own material, so self-absorption is included naturally.

A.4  Source meshing

Sources are subdivided so finite extent and internal self-shielding are integrated rather than treated as a single point. Each element gets activity proportional to its volume, positioned at the cell centroid and mapped through the volume's rotation:

  • Cylinder / Disk — cylindrical grid (radial shells × angular sectors × axial slabs); element volume ½(ro²−ri²)·Δθ·Δz.
  • Box — Cartesian grid; element volume Δx·Δy·Δz.
  • Sphere — spherical grid with equal-cos φ polar bands (equal solid angle); element volume ⅓(ro³−ri³)(cos φ₁−cos φ₂)·Δθ.

Total meshed activity exactly equals the source activity (regression-tested). Dose-point mesh presets: Coarse 3×6×6, Medium 5×8×10, Fine 8×12×16, Very Fine 12×16×24 (per source). Isodose generation uses a fixed 5×16×8 = 640 elements per source.

Appendix BBuildup factors & the GP form

Exponential attenuation alone counts only uncollided photons. In thick shields, photons that scatter one or more times still reach the dose point with degraded energy. The buildup factor B ≥ 1 corrects for this scattered component and is the physics that separates a realistic estimate from a low one.

B.1  The Geometric-Progression (GP) form

RadShield uses the GP fitting form standardized in ANSI/ANS-6.4.3-1991 (point isotropic source, infinite homogeneous medium):

B(E,x) = 1 + (b−1)(Kˣ−1)/(K−1) if K ≠ 1 B(E,x) = 1 + (b−1)·x if K = 1 K(x) = c·xᵃ + d·[tanh(x/X_k − 2) − tanh(−2)] / [1 − tanh(−2)]

where x is depth in mean free paths and {b, c, a, d, X_k} are the tabulated GP coefficients per material per energy. b is the 1-mfp buildup factor and K(x) is the dose-multiplication ratio per mfp. The fits are valid to x ≤ 40 mfp over 0.015–15 MeV.

B.2  Coefficient provenance

  • Exposure buildup coefficient sets for water, iron (used for carbon steel), and concrete — full 0.015–15 MeV grids (25 energies) — and lead — 0.03–15 MeV including the K-edge fine structure (31 energies, with the 0.088/0.089 MeV discontinuity) — transcribed from ANSI/ANS-6.4.3-1991, Table 5.1.
  • Air uses the standard's energy-absorption GP set (the 1991 standard publishes no exposure set for air; for low-Z media the two differ by <~2%, and air is never the dominant buildup medium here).
  • Coefficients are interpolated between grid energies linearly in ln E (each of b, c, a, d, X_k independently); the lead K-edge is handled by the dense grid rather than special-casing.
  • Verification: the buildup module recomputes 28 factors and compares against the standard's Table 3 — all within 2% (e.g., lead B(1 MeV, 10 mfp) = 3.50 vs 3.51; iron 15.7 vs 15.8; water 26.4 vs 26.1). See §5 and Appendix G.

B.3  Multi-layer shields — the "outermost material" method

GP data is defined for a single homogeneous medium; real shield stacks (water → steel → lead → air) need an engineering rule. RadShield uses the standard MicroShield-style approximation:

Compute the total mean free paths x through all layers, then evaluate B(E,x) with the GP coefficients of the last significant material along the ray — the material nearest the dose point with non-negligible optical thickness (mfp > 0.001), air excluded.

Rationale: the scattered-photon spectrum arriving at the dose point is shaped predominantly by the last shield it traversed. This is exact for a single material, good when the outer layer is ≥~2 mfp, and least accurate for a thin high-Z outer liner over a thick low-Z shield (it will typically under-estimate buildup there). If no significant non-air layer exists, B = 1.

Appendix CPoint-kernel equations, step by step

For one source element (activity A Ci) and one gamma line (energy E MeV, yield y photons/disintegration), at distance r cm through layers i:

Step 1 — emission rate

S = A × 3.7×10¹⁰ × y [photons/s]

Step 2 — uncollided fluence rate (inverse square)

φ_unc = S / (4π r²) [photons/(cm²·s)]

Step 3 — attenuation: total mean free paths

x = Σᵢ µᵢ(E)·tᵢ [dimensionless, "mfp"] µᵢ(E) = (µ/ρ)ᵢ(E) · ρᵢ [cm⁻¹]

(µ/ρ)ᵢ(E) is the total mass attenuation coefficient from NIST XCOM (Appendix D), log-log interpolated in energy; ρᵢ is the density. If Include air attenuation is off, air layers are skipped in this sum.

Step 4 — buildup factor B(E,x) — see Appendix B, applied with the coefficients of the outermost significant material.

Step 5 — attenuated fluence with buildup

φ = φ_unc · B(E,x) · e⁻ˣ

Step 6 — fluence → dose rate, using the mass energy-absorption coefficient of air:

Ḋ = φ · E · (µ_en/ρ)_air(E) · C C = 1.602×10⁻⁶ [erg/MeV] × 3600 [s/hr] × 1000 [mrad/rad] ÷ 100 [erg/(g·rad)] = 5.767×10⁻² [mrad/hr per (photon/(cm²·s))·MeV·(cm²/g)]

With quality factor 1 for photons, mrad/hr is reported as mrem/hr; mSv/hr = mrem/hr ÷ 100.

Step 7 — summation. The scene dose rate is the double sum over all source elements and all gamma lines, each element ray-traced independently and carrying the isotope of the source volume it came from — so mixed-isotope scenes transport every source with its own gamma lines. If r < 0.01 cm (dose point inside a source element) the result is reported as infinite.

C.1  Unshielded quick-reference

The results panel also shows a bare point-source check from the isotope's specific gamma-ray constant Γ:

Ẋ [R/hr] = Γ · A / d² (A in Ci, d in meters)

summed per enabled source with its own Γ and distance. It is not used in the main calculation — it is the sanity check and the basis of the displayed "Shielding Factor" (unshielded ÷ calculated).

Appendix EIsodose, heat maps & time simulation

E.1  Isodose surfaces

To draw a surface of constant dose rate, each enabled source provides one ray-cast vantage at its element centroid; dose evaluated anywhere still sums all sources with full ray-traced shielding — the vantage only controls the search topology. Ray directions come from a subdivided icosahedron (162 / 642 / 2562 near-uniform directions), which also triangulates the surface. Along each ray, the code marches outward from 1 cm in ×1.5 steps until the dose first drops below the level, then bisects — taking the first crossing so a vantage's surface stays on its own dose lobe rather than tunneling to another source's far lobe. Each (level × source) pair renders as its own translucent mesh with distance labels and a double-click inspector. Isodose is a visualization aid; the dose-point calculation with its finer mesh is the number of record.

E.2  Survey heat map

The current source mesh is evaluated with the full ray-traced point-kernel at every node of a horizontal grid (user height / spacing / extent, capped at 121×121) and painted onto a plane with a fixed logarithmic color scale (one decade per band, 0.1 → 10⁴ mrem/hr) so maps compare across scenes. It is the dose-point calculation repeated ~10³ times — no additional approximation beyond a coarser mesh.

E.3  Parameter sweep

The chosen dimension is set to each value in the range, the sources are re-meshed, and the standard calculation runs at every dose point; the original dimension is restored afterward. Each step is an honest full calculation — no caching.

E.4  Time simulation

With fixed geometry, dose rate is linear in each source's activity. The engine precomputes a dose-per-curie matrix D[source][point] (one full ray-traced calculation per source per dose point), after which the dose at any time is dose_j(t) = Σ_i A_i(t)·D[i][j] and an entire timeline evaluates in microseconds. Per timestep it tracks: transfers between sources along a chosen curve (linear, S-curve, front- or back-loaded), an optional carrier/hose transit inventory, optional radioactive decay (2−t/T½ per isotope), and spills (a puddle that grows as r(t) = √(V·F(t)/(π·depth)), its dose recomputed per step because its geometry changes). Outputs per dose point: the dose-rate time series, the peak (value and time), and the integrated dose (trapezoidal, mrem) — the dose budget for the evolution.

Appendix FFull approximations & limitations

  1. Point-kernel + buildup, not transport. Buildup assumes an infinite homogeneous medium; finite or lopsided shields, streaming paths, skyshine, corner effects, and maze scatter are not modeled.
  2. Outermost-material buildup for layered shields (§B.3) — the dominant remaining approximation now that the GP data matches the standard. Worst case: thin high-Z outer layer over thick low-Z shield.
  3. Coherent-scattering inconsistency (small). The standard's buildup factors were computed neglecting coherent scattering, so the mfp argument should strictly use µ-without-coherent; RadShield uses XCOM totals throughout (<1% above ~0.3 MeV, a few percent at very low energies).
  4. Gamma only, QF = 1. No beta, neutron, bremsstrahlung, or X-ray; exposure-in-air response, not ICRP organ/effective dose.
  5. Mesh discretization. Within ~one element size of a source surface, finite meshing over/under-shoots; increase mesh density for near-contact points.
  6. No automatic decay during a session unless a time simulation with decay is run — activities are as entered.
  7. Bundled five materials at fixed density/composition; steel is pure-iron data.
  8. Energy range 0.01–10 MeV (attenuation) and 0.015–15 MeV (buildup); bundled isotopes stay inside both.
  9. Simulation bookkeeping: decay is applied to post-transfer inventories; transferred activity adopts the destination's isotope; the spill puddle's self-shielding effect on other sources is not re-traced per step.

  • isodose_check — 15 checks: two-separated-source scenario, lobe radii, drawn surface points within 10% of the requested level, dumbbell merging, mixed-isotope transport.
